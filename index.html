Part 1

Cultural Overview by Region

Understanding AU/NZ and SEA communication styles

Why Pick Up ANZ & SEA Among GE Countries

Time Zone Overlap

We are in closer time zones with ANZ and SEA countries, which means we have greater overlap in working hours and can collaborate more effectively in real time.

Benefit When You Support US

ANZ customers are closer to US customers in terms of communication style and expectations — direct, outcome‑focused, and ownership‑driven — though ANZ places stronger emphasis on work‑life boundaries.

Australia / New Zealand (Global English)

Straightforward & Candid

They'll tell you directly what's wrong — no need to read between the lines

Strong Work-Life Balance

Don't expect replies outside business hours, and they won't expect yours either

Casual Tone Preferred

Overly formal language feels stiff; friendly and approachable works best

Results Over Process

They care more about "does it work?" than perfect procedures

Egalitarian Culture

Titles and hierarchy matter less; first-name basis is the norm

Self-Sufficient

Often arrive with preliminary investigation already done

Humor Helps

Light humor builds rapport (but keep it appropriate)

Southeast Asia (SEA)

Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines, Vietnam, and others

Relationship-First

Building personal rapport is essential before diving into technical work — small talk matters

Indirect Communication

Many SEA cultures avoid confrontation; "yes" may mean "I heard you" not "I agree"

Respect for Hierarchy

Titles and seniority are important; be polite and defer to senior stakeholders

Patience & Flexibility

Decision-making can be slower due to internal consensus processes; avoid rushing them

Language Nuances

English proficiency varies widely (Singapore/Philippines = strong, others may need simpler language)

Vendor Loyalty

Tend to be loyal once trust is established — long-term relationship matters more than one-time speed

Non-Confrontational

Even when unhappy, they may not express it directly — watch for subtle signals.
E.g., Silence can be a warning sign — when it happens, rethink the approach instead of following a mechanical follow‑up.

Cultural Sensitivity

Be mindful of local holidays (Ramadan, Lunar New Year, Deepavali) and prayer times

Part 2

Key Tips for Handling
AU/NZ & SEA Cases

8 tips for AU/NZ + 5 tips for SEA

Tips for AU/NZ Customers

1

Tone Down the Formality

  • Don't: Write long, overly polite emails with excessive honorifics
  • Do: Use a friendly, conversational tone — "Hi John, here's a quick update…"
  • AU/NZ customers feel uncomfortable if the tone is too formal
2

Be Direct — Don't Hint, Just Say It

  • JP/KR engineers are trained to communicate indirectly to be polite
  • AU/NZ customers prefer clarity: "This is a product bug, ETA is 2 weeks"
  • If you don't have an answer: "I don't have an answer yet, I'll update you by Friday"
3

Keep Emails Short

  • Lead with the conclusion/action item first, details second
  • Use bullet points, not paragraphs
  • Rule of thumb: If your email scrolls, it's too long
4

Don't Over-Apologize

  • In JP/KR culture, frequent apologies show respect
  • In AU/NZ, excessive apologies feel insincere or weak
  • One clear apology is enough — then move to the solution

Tips for AU/NZ Customers

5

Respect Work-Life Boundaries

  • Don't send "urgent" emails at 9 PM their time expecting a reply
  • Don't call outside of their working hours
  • Schedule meetings within their business hours (AEST/NZST)
6

Don't Be Afraid of Casual Interaction

  • AU/NZ may joke, use slang ("no worries", "cheers", "mate")
  • This is rapport-building, not unprofessional — reciprocate appropriately
  • A friendly "Thanks, cheers!" at the end of an email is totally fine
7

Focus on Outcomes, Not Process

  • JP/KR engineers may want to explain every step of investigation
  • AU/NZ customers care about: "What's the fix? When will it be done?"
  • Share process details only when asked, or put them in an attachment
8

Treat Them as Equals

  • Use their first name directly
  • Disagreeing respectfully is welcome: "I think the root cause might be X…"
  • They respect engineers who show confidence and ownership

Tips for SEA Customers

1

Invest in Relationship Building

  • Spend a few minutes on small talk before jumping into technical details
  • Remember previous conversations: "How did that migration go last month?"
  • Don't treat each interaction as purely transactional
2

Read Between the Lines

  • Silence or vague responses may indicate disagreement or confusion
  • "Yes" might mean "I understand" rather than "I agree"
  • If a customer goes quiet, proactively check in
  • Avoid putting them in a position to say "no" directly; offer alternatives
3

Adjust Language Complexity

  • Use short sentences, avoid idioms (no "ballpark figure", "touch base", "circle back")
  • Use screenshots, diagrams, and step-by-step visuals to supplement
  • If there's a language gap on a call, ask short, clear questions and follow up with written summary

Tips for SEA Customers

4

Be Patient with Decision Cycles

  • SEA organizations may need to consult multiple internal stakeholders before approving actions
  • Don't interpret slow responses as disinterest — they may be building internal consensus
  • Offer to provide written materials they can share internally
  • Set clear next-step timelines to keep momentum without pressuring
5

Respect Religious & Cultural Calendars

  • Malaysia/Indonesia: Ramadan, Hari Raya, Friday prayer times
  • Thailand: Buddhist holidays; Songkran (Thai New Year) in April
  • Philippines: Strong Christmas season (Sep–Jan may see reduced availability)
  • Singapore: Multi-cultural — Lunar New Year, Deepavali, Hari Raya
  • When scheduling, ask: "Is there a preferred time that works best for you?"

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

❌ JP/KR Habit ✅ AU/NZ Expectation ✅ SEA Expectation
"We sincerely apologize for the inconvenience caused…" (x3) "Sorry about that — here's what we're doing about it" One polite apology is fine; they appreciate the effort
2-page detailed status email 5-line summary + "let me know if you want details" Moderate detail with clear next steps
Waiting for perfect answer before replying Quick acknowledgment + follow-up with details later Acknowledgment appreciated; they're patient but want to know you're working on it
Avoiding saying "I don't know" "Not sure yet, investigating — will update by [date]" "I'm looking into this" — honesty is valued, but frame it gently
Asking manager before every response Take ownership, make decisions, escalate only when needed Ownership valued, but they understand if you need to consult internally
Being too indirect or vague — hinting instead of stating
e.g. “It might be difficult to proceed…” (meaning: it’s not possible)
“We will do our best to consider…” (meaning: probably no)
“There may be some challenges…” (meaning: there’s a blocker) 		Just say it: “This isn’t possible because X. Here’s what we can do instead.” Soften your language, but still be clear on the outcome and next steps

Overall

For AU/NZ

Be direct, be brief, be friendly,
and own the problem.

For SEA

Be warm, be patient, build trust,
and read between the lines.

Universal Principle

Both regions value authenticity — just expressed differently.
Regardless of region, clear expectation-setting and proactive progress updates
are the keys to earning customer trust.

Q3: Working hours in AU/NZ may differ (e.g., 8-4). With time zone differences, how do we handle the short overlap?
— KR DSC (Man Woo)

Key Facts

  • Many Australians work 8:00–16:00 or 8:30–16:30
  • AEST (Sydney, Melbourne) = UTC+10, NZST (Auckland) = UTC+12
  • Daylight time:
    ~ Oct ~ April, AEDT = UTC+11
    ~ Sep ~ April, NZDT = UTC+13
  • Use worldtimebuddy.com to align multiple time zones for meetings

Practical Tips

  • Calculate your time difference (how many hours) at very early stage.
  • Schedule calls and send emails during your morning hours.
  • During your morning hours, put ANZ customer with higher priority to reply.
  • Use the time difference as an advantage: you investigate during your day, they review during theirs
